Responsible Disclosure

How to Report a Vulnerability

The 60 seconds after finding a vulnerability determine everything. This guide is the difference between a criminal charge and a career-defining recognition — written for Indian security researchers.

🚫
India has no safe harbour for security researchers. Unlike the US (CFAA reform proposals), India's IT Act 2000 has no explicit exemption for ethical hacking. Section 66 (unauthorized access) carries 3 years imprisonment + ₹5 lakh fine. Section 70 (protected systems — any government portal) carries 10 years. Your only protection is a paper trail showing you followed responsible disclosure before touching anything.

Step-by-Step Disclosure Process

1
Stop — document minimally
Take 1–2 screenshots proving the vulnerability exists. Record a short screen capture if needed. Do NOT: download bulk data, access more than 1–2 records as proof, run automated scanners, or return to the system multiple times. The moment you have proof, stop.
⚠ Every extra record you access is extra evidence against you. One screenshot proving an IDOR exists is identical legal proof to downloading 27 million records — but only one of them is criminal.
2
Write a professional report
A clear, technical, professional report gets taken seriously and patched faster. A threatening or demanding message gets forwarded to lawyers.
Report template
Subject: Security Vulnerability Report — [Vulnerability Type] on [domain] Summary: I discovered a [vulnerability class] on [URL/endpoint] that allows [impact — e.g. "any unauthenticated user to access other students' data"]. Steps to reproduce: 1. Navigate to https://example.gov.in/endpoint 2. [Exact steps] 3. [Expected vs actual behaviour] Proof of concept: [Attached screenshot showing the vulnerability] [NO bulk data — just enough to prove the bug exists] Severity: High / Critical — [brief justification based on CVSS factors] Suggested fix: [Your recommendation — parameterized queries, server-side check, etc.] My details: Name: [Your name] Email: [Your email] I am reporting this in good faith and have not shared this information with any third party. I request acknowledgement within 7 days and a patch within 90 days. Regards, [Name]
3
Send to the right channel
Different targets, different channels. Always CC CERT-In for Indian government targets — it creates an official timestamp that you reported responsibly.
🏠 Government / NIC portals
Primary: incident@cert-in.org.in
CC: ciso-nic@nic.in
CC: it@cbse.gov.in (for CBSE)
CC: info@nciipc.gov.in (critical infra)
🏢 Private companies
Check: security.txt at /.well-known/security.txt
Check: HackerOne / Bugcrowd program
Fallback: security@ or ciso@ email
Fallback: LinkedIn to find CISO
🌐 International platforms
Most large tech companies have bug bounty programs on HackerOne or Bugcrowd. Facebook, Google, Apple, Microsoft all pay ₹8L–₹1Cr+ for critical bugs.
📜 No channel exists?
Email the domain's WHOIS admin contact. If no response in 14 days, escalate to CERT-In. They can coordinate with the organization on your behalf.
4
Wait — follow up — be patient
Industry standard is 90 days from initial report to public disclosure. Government systems may need longer — be flexible if they're actively working on a patch.
Day 1
Send initial report. Keep timestamp evidence (email sent receipt).
Day 7
Follow up if no acknowledgement. One polite email is fine.
Day 30
Second follow-up if still no response. CC CERT-In if not already done.
Day 90
Standard disclosure window ends. Discuss public disclosure timeline — but for Indian govt targets, consult a lawyer first.
Patched
Organisation confirms fix. You may now publish a write-up (no exploit code, no sensitive data). This builds your security portfolio.
5
Keep all records — forever
Screenshot every email. Export your sent mail. Keep a dated log of every action you took. If you are ever questioned by police or lawyers, your paper trail showing good-faith disclosure is your strongest legal protection — as demonstrated by the CBSE case.

Do vs Don't — Quick Reference

✔ Ethical — safe to do
  • ✔ Test on your own accounts only
  • ✔ Use public passive tools (securityheaders.com, SSL Labs)
  • ✔ View page source and HTTP headers in your browser
  • ✔ Inspect JS bundles on pages you are viewing
  • ✔ Report immediately after finding proof
  • ✔ Keep all communication professional and documented
  • ✔ Write a public post-patch write-up (no exploit code)
  • ✔ Apply for bug bounty reward if one exists
❌ Criminal — never do
  • ❌ Access other users' accounts or data
  • ❌ Run automated scanners on live government systems
  • ❌ Download bulk data as "proof"
  • ❌ Share vulnerability details on WhatsApp / Twitter before patch
  • ❌ Demand money or threaten to publish
  • ❌ Sell unpatched vulnerabilities
  • ❌ Return to the system after initial proof
  • ❌ Share credentials / session tokens you found
